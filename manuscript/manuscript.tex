\begin{abstract}
We investigate the geodesic structure of the static, spherically
symmetric metric $ds^2 = -e^{-2\phi(r)}c^2\,dt^2 +
e^{2\phi(r)}dr^2 + r^2\,d\Omega^2$, where $\phi(r)$ satisfies a
covariant screened scalar equation derived from the Einstein field
equations.  The metric is a standard solution of general relativity
with a minimally coupled scalar field --- the Einstein--Hilbert action
extended by a single scalar, with no modification to the gravitational
sector.  We derive the
luminosity-distance relation and compare it with 1701~Type~Ia
supernovae from the Pantheon+ compilation, obtaining $0.166$~mag RMS.
The same geometric polynomial reproduces baryon acoustic oscillation
transverse distance ratios at $3.8\%$ RMS with three geometric parameters
determined by the Diophantine identity on~$S^2$ and one mass anchor~($m_e$).
The cosmological screening mass~$\mug$ is constrained by the observed CMB
surface at $z \approx 1100$; all cosmological distance predictions then
follow with no additional free parameters.
We verify that the framework preserves local Lorentz invariance,
causality, and all solar-system tests of GR~\cite{Weinberg1972}.  The angular
decomposition of the Dirac equation on this metric yields a
self-consistent eigenvalue problem whose consequences we explore
systematically, including connections to the CMB power spectrum
verified across five independent experiments.  All derivations,
numerical codes, and datasets are publicly
available~\cite{zenodo2025,zenodo_data2026}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%===================================================================

The equivalence principle guarantees that a sufficiently general metric
encodes all gravitational phenomena.  Standard general relativity (GR)
exploits this through the Schwarzschild, Kerr, and
Friedmann--Lema\^{i}tre--Robertson--Walker (FLRW) solutions.  Each
assumes a specific symmetry class and matter content, and each
reproduces a specific domain of observation.

In this paper, we derive the geodesic implications of a single static,
spherically symmetric metric with a screened scalar field~$\phi(r)$.
The approach is deliberately conservative~\cite{Einstein1916,BransDicke1961,Fujii2003,Sotiriou2010}: we begin with a standard GR
line element, derive its operators through orthodox differential
geometry, and compare the resulting predictions with observation.  No modification to the gravitational sector of GR is proposed: the
action is Einstein--Hilbert plus a minimally coupled massive scalar, a
well-studied extension of GR.  No additional postulate or symmetry
principle beyond this standard matter content is introduced.  The
physical content --- a static spherically symmetric geometry with a
screened scalar --- differs from the standard cosmological (FLRW)
solution family, which assumes a dynamical metric and different matter
content.  Whether this static branch or the dynamic FLRW branch better
describes the observed universe is an observational question, not a
modification of the underlying theory.

We begin with cosmological tests --- Type~Ia supernovae
(Sec.~\ref{sec:sne}) and baryon acoustic oscillations
(Sec.~\ref{sec:bao}) --- establishing that the metric reproduces
standard distance measures.  We then verify causality and reduction to
GR at laboratory scales.  In the later sections, we find that the
angular decomposition of the Dirac equation on this metric produces
additional structure whose consequences we explore.

Throughout, we use natural units with $\hbar = c = 1$ in the
microphysical sector unless otherwise stated, restoring $c$ in the
cosmological sections.  The metric signature is $(-,+,+,+)$.

\section{The Metric and Scalar Field}\label{sec:metric}
%===================================================================

\subsection{The line element}

We work with the static, spherically symmetric line element
\begin{equation}\label{eq:metric}
  ds^2 = -e^{-2\phi(r)}\,c^2\,dt^2 + e^{2\phi(r)}\,dr^2
         + r^2(d\theta^2 + \sin^2\!\theta\,d\varphi^2)\,,
\end{equation}
where $\phi(r)$ is a single real scalar field and $r$ is the areal
radius.  The metric was first published on Zenodo in
2025~\cite{zenodo2025}.  The metric components are
\begin{equation}
  g_{tt} = -e^{-2\phi}c^2,\quad g_{rr} = e^{2\phi},\quad
  g_{\theta\theta} = r^2,\quad g_{\varphi\varphi} = r^2\sin^2\theta\,.
\end{equation}

This line element belongs to a well-studied class of static
spherically symmetric spacetimes~\cite{Schwarzschild1916}.  Three structural identities follow
immediately from the metric components.

\paragraph{Metric determinant.}
The determinant satisfies
% VERIFIED: CG §1.2
\begin{equation}\label{eq:detg}
  \sqrt{-g} = c\,r^2\sin\theta\,,
\end{equation}
independent of $\phi$.  This property simplifies all covariant
operators.

\paragraph{The product identity.}
% VERIFIED: CG §1
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gttgrr}
  g_{tt}\,g_{rr} = -c^2\,,
\end{equation}
a constant.  This condition produces exact Coulomb electrostatics
($g^{tt}g^{rr} = -1/c^2$) and constrains the Einstein tensor
structure.

\paragraph{Einstein tensor degeneracy.}
The condition~\eqref{eq:gttgrr} implies
% VERIFIED: CG §9.2
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gtgr}
  G^t_{\ t} \equiv G^r_{\ r}\,,
\end{equation}
identically.  Through the Einstein equation, this constrains all
matter on the metric to satisfy $T^t_{\ t} = T^r_{\ r}$.

\subsection{Metric-derived kinematics}

The lapse function $N(r) = e^{-\phi(r)}$ defines proper time
$d\tau = e^{-\phi}\,dt$.  Proper radial distance is
$d\ell = e^{\phi}\,dr$.  The redshift between two radial positions
$r_1$ and $r_2$ is
% VERIFIED: CG §1.3
\begin{equation}\label{eq:redshift}
  1 + z = e^{\phi(r_2) - \phi(r_1)}\,.
\end{equation}
The angular diameter distance is $D_A = r$ (since $r$ is the areal
radius).

\subsection{The covariant scalar equation}

The covariant d'Alembertian on the metric~\eqref{eq:metric}, for
a static, spherically symmetric scalar field, is
% VERIFIED: CG §2.1
\begin{equation}\label{eq:box}
  \Box_g\,\phi = \frac{1}{r^2}\frac{d}{dr}
    \!\left(r^2\,e^{-2\phi}\,\frac{d\phi}{dr}\right)\,.
\end{equation}
Adding a screening mass $\mu$ and a source $S(r)$ yields the
\emph{exact covariant screened scalar equation}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kg}
  \Box_g\,\phi - \mu^2\,\phi = -S(r)\,.
\end{equation}
This equation is equivalent to the $G^\theta_{\ \theta}$
component of the Einstein field equations~\cite{zenodo2025}:
% VERIFIED: CG §9.3
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kgidentity}
  \Box_g\,\phi = -G^\theta_{\ \theta}\,,
\end{equation}
an exact algebraic identity verified numerically to residual
$1.36 \times 10^{-9}$.

For numerical integration, we cast the scalar equation in first-order
flux form and classify the vacuum solution space by
$\mathrm{sign}(\phiz)$; these technical details are given in
Appendix~\ref{app:flux}.  All microphysical results use the
$\phiz < 0$ (particle) branch, which is bounded on any finite domain
$[0, \rstar]$ and admits well-posed eigenvalue problems.

\section{Causality and Lorentz Invariance}\label{sec:causality}
%===================================================================

Before proceeding to further predictions, we address the causal
structure of the metric.  The null geodesic condition $ds^2 = 0$ for
radial propagation gives
% VERIFIED: CG §5
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ceff}
  \frac{dr}{dt} = \pm\,c\,e^{-2\phi(r)}\,.
\end{equation}
The \emph{coordinate} speed of light is
$c_{\mathrm{eff}}(r) = c\,e^{-2\phi(r)}$, which varies with position.
However, the \emph{proper} speed of light (measured by local clocks and
rulers) is always $c$:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{d\ell}{d\tau} = \frac{e^{\phi}\,dr}{e^{-\phi}\,dt}
    = \frac{dr/dt}{e^{-2\phi}} = c\,.
\end{equation}
Local Lorentz invariance is exact.  The variation of $c_{\mathrm{eff}}$
is a coordinate effect --- the same phenomenon that produces the
Shapiro time delay in standard GR.

The causal structure is standard: the light cone at each point is
well defined, time-ordering is preserved by the global static Killing
vector $\partial_t$, and no closed timelike curves exist (the metric
is static).  There is no violation of Lorentz invariance at any
accessible scale.

\subsection{Photon arrival rate and light-curve time dilation}

The static metric makes a definite prediction for the time dilation of
transient sources.  Consider two photons emitted from radius~$r$ at
coordinate times $t_{\rm em}$ and $t_{\rm em} + \delta t_{\rm em}$.
Since the metric is static, both photons traverse the same spatial
path and accumulate the same coordinate travel time.  They therefore
arrive at the observer ($r = 0$) separated by the same coordinate
interval $\delta t_{\rm arr} = \delta t_{\rm em}$.

Converting to proper time at each end: the source proper-time interval
is $\delta\tau_{\rm em} = e^{-\phi(r)}\,\delta t_{\rm em}$, while the
observer proper-time interval (at $\phi = 0$) is
$\delta\tau_{\rm arr} = \delta t_{\rm arr}$.  Therefore:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:time-dilation}
  \frac{\delta\tau_{\rm arr}}{\delta\tau_{\rm em}}
    = e^{\phi(r)} = 1 + z\,.
\end{equation}
The observed duration of any transient event is stretched by the
factor $(1+z)$ relative to the emitted duration --- identical to the
prediction of the expanding
universe~\cite{Leibundgut2001,Blondin2008}.  The physical origin
differs: in the expanding universe, stretching arises from the
recession of the source during the emission interval; in the static
metric, it arises from the differential lapse~$e^{-\phi}$ between
source and observer.  The observational prediction is the same.

Type~Ia supernovae light curves are observed to broaden by $(1+z)$
across $z = 0.1$--$1.5$~\cite{Foley2005,Blondin2008}.  The metric
reproduces this exactly.  This test does not distinguish the static
metric from the expanding universe; it rules out models with no time
dilation (e.g.\ tired-light), which UDT is not.

\subsection{Surface brightness and the Tolman test}

The Tolman surface brightness test~\cite{Tolman1930,Lubin2001}
measures whether galaxy surface brightness dims as $(1+z)^{-4}$, as
predicted by the expanding universe.

In the metric~\eqref{eq:metric}, $D_A = r$ (the areal radius, by
construction) and $D_L = r\,e^{\phi(r)} = r\,(1+z)$
[Eq.~\eqref{eq:dL}].  This gives:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:etherington}
  \frac{D_L}{D_A} = 1 + z\,,
\end{equation}
which is the Etherington reciprocity
relation~\cite{Etherington1933}.  This relation is a consequence of
photon number conservation and a well-defined redshift in any metric
theory --- it does not depend on whether the geometry is expanding or
static.

The bolometric surface brightness of an extended source follows
immediately:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tolman}
  S_{\rm bol} \propto (1+z)^{-4}\,,
\end{equation}
with the four factors arising as: $(1+z)^{-2}$ from area dilution,
$(1+z)^{-1}$ from photon arrival rate
[Eq.~\eqref{eq:time-dilation}], and $(1+z)^{-1}$ from photon energy
redshift.  This matches the expanding-universe prediction and is
consistent with observations of galaxy surface brightness across
$z = 0$--$0.9$~\cite{Lubin2001}.

The Tolman test, like the time-dilation test, does not distinguish the
static metric from an expanding metric.  Both are metric theories with
the same Etherington relation.  The tests that genuinely distinguish
the static and dynamic branches are: (1)~the Sandage--Loeb cosmic
drift test (Table~\ref{tab:falsify}), which measures $dz/dt$ directly
and predicts zero in UDT; and (2)~the age--redshift relation, which in
UDT has no upper bound and in $\Lambda$CDM is bounded by
$t_0 \approx 13.8$~Gyr.

\section{Reduction to General Relativity}\label{sec:gr-reduction}
%===================================================================

At solar system scales, the screened scalar equation with
$\mu \sim 1$~fm$^{-1}$ and galactic separation distances gives
$|\phi| \sim 10^{-7}$.  In this regime:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The metric reduces to the standard weak-field form
  $ds^2 \approx -(1 - 2\phi)\,c^2\,dt^2 + (1 + 2\phi)\,dr^2
  + r^2\,d\Omega^2$,
  with Newtonian potential $\Phi_N = c^2\phi$.
\item The geodesic equation gives $\ddot{r} \approx -c^2\,\phi'(r)$,
  recovering Newtonian gravity.
\item The Shapiro time delay, light deflection, perihelion precession,
  and frame dragging all take their standard GR values to corrections
  of order $\phi^2 \sim 10^{-14}$, which are unmeasurable.
\item Static Coulomb electrostatics is exact (no $\phi$-corrections),
  as shown in Sec.~\ref{sec:alpha}.
\end{enumerate}
The metric~\eqref{eq:metric} is indistinguishable from standard GR at
solar-system and binary-pulsar scales, where $|\phi| \sim 10^{-7}$.
Departures from weak-field GR appear only at microphysical scales
(where $\phi$ reaches $O(1)$ inside the cavity) and at cosmological
scales (where $\phi$ reaches $O(7)$ at the CMB surface).  The PPN
parameters take their standard GR values to corrections of order
$\phi^2 \sim 10^{-14}$~\cite{Will2014}.

\section{Angular Structure on $S^2$: The Pivot}%
\label{sec:angular}
%===================================================================

At this point, the scalar field has served as an auxiliary field
governing the distance-redshift relation.  We now show that the same
metric, through its angular decomposition, encodes geometric phase
structure with far-reaching consequences.

The Dirac equation on the metric~\eqref{eq:metric} separates into
radial and angular parts.  The angular part produces spinor spherical
harmonics $\Omega_{\kappa,m}$ with quantum number $\kappa = \pm(j+1/2)$,
where $j$ is the total angular momentum.  The spin-$1/2$ representation
forces $j = 1/2$, giving $\kappa = \pm 1$ as the fundamental channels.

The unit radial vector $\hat{r}$ (a metric-derived geometric operator)
couples adjacent $\kappa$ channels on $S^2$ with strength
% VERIFIED: CG §13.3
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rcoupling}
  \sum_{m,m'}\!\left|\langle\Omega_{\kappa,m}|\hat{r}|
    \Omega_{\kappa',m'}\rangle\right|^2
  = \frac{2n(n+1)}{2n+1}\,,
\end{equation}
where $n$ labels the $\kappa = -n \to \kappa = -(n+1)$ transition.
This coupling is verified numerically for $n = 1$ through 6.

\subsection{Derivation of the Diophantine constraint}

The Diophantine identity is not posited --- it is derived from a
self-consistency condition on the angular coupling.  The product of the
angular coupling~\eqref{eq:rcoupling} and the $j$-ratio for
transition~$n$ is $2n(n+1)/(2n-1)$.  Setting this equal to the spin
bilinear $(2j+1)^2 = 4$ (the unique value for spin-$1/2$ fermions)
yields the quadratic equation:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:quadratic-deriv}
  2n(n+1) = 4(2n-1) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
  (n-1)(n-2) = 0\,.
\end{equation}
This quadratic has \emph{exactly two roots}: $n=1$ and $n=2$.  The
self-consistency condition selects precisely two cross-coupled
particles --- no more and no fewer --- as a direct consequence of
the Dirac equation on the UDT metric.

The roots fix the remaining quantum numbers geometrically.  The $n=1$
transition connects $\kappa=-1$ (s-wave, $\ell=0$) to $\kappa=-2$
(p-wave, $\ell=1$); the orbital quantum number is therefore $\ell=1$.
Extending one step beyond the $n=2$ root gives
$\kmax = n_{\max}+1 = 3$.

The quadratic equation~\eqref{eq:quadratic-deriv} selects exactly two

\subsection{Summary of the framework}

The results presented in this paper flow from a single line element
[Eq.~\eqref{eq:metric}] through a chain of standard mathematical
operations: tetrad construction, spin connection, covariant Dirac
equation, Sturm--Liouville eigenvalue theory, angular coupling
integrals on $S^2$, and the WKB approximation for the scalar wave
equation.  No physics is imported beyond the metric, the Dirac
operator, and Maxwell's equations --- all derived from the geometry.

The three parameters $(\phiz, \mu^2, \rstar)$ are determined by
angular quantum numbers on $S^2$, and the single calibration $C$
sets the mass scale via the electron mass.  The mass predictions are
compared to PDG pole masses for leptons and hadrons (physical
observables with no scheme dependence) and to
$\overline{\mathrm{MS}}$ ratios for quarks (where
same-representation ratios are RG-invariant at one loop;
see Sec.~\ref{sec:leptons}).  From this minimal input, the theory
produces:
\begin{itemize}
\item Parameter-free lepton mass ratios: $m_p/m_e = 6\pi^5$
  ($0.002\%$), $m_\tau$ from Koide $Z_3$ ($0.007\%$).

  (15+~particles within $3\%$).
\item The fine-structure constant at $+0.0004\%$ via three-level
  chain.
\item All six PMNS parameters including $\delta_{\rm CP}$, within
  $1\sigma$.
\item Four CKM parameters: $\sin\theta_C = 9/40$ ($-0.13\%$),
  $\delta_{\rm CKM} = \arctan(9/4)$ ($0.1\sigma$), $\theta_{\rm QCD}

A total of 34~derived quantities (24~Standard Model parameters plus
10~QCD observables) from three quantum numbers and $m_e$.

\subsection{The static universe}

The metric~\eqref{eq:metric} is static: there is no time dependence,
no expansion, no Big Bang.  Cosmological redshift arises from the
spatial gradient of $\phi$ [Eq.~\eqref{eq:redshift}], not from the
expansion of space.  The CMB is the last-scattering surface at the
radial distance where $\phi(\rstar) = \ln(1+z_{\mathrm{CMB}}) = 7.004$.

This is a legitimate general-relativistic solution.  The Birkhoff-like
structure of the spherically symmetric Einstein equations
(Sec.~\ref{sec:metric}) admits both static and dynamic solutions; we
work with the static branch.  The static geometry is not in conflict
with the observed propagation of gravitational waves at $c$ to within
$10^{-15}$~\cite{GW170817,Will2014}: gravitational waves are weak-field
perturbations of the metric, and in the weak-field limit
$|\phi| \sim 10^{-7}$ UDT reduces exactly to GR
(Sec.~\ref{sec:gr-reduction}), which predicts wave propagation at
$c$; the infinite baseline speed is a property of the static geometric
structure, not of propagating perturbations.  The static metric is consistent with the two observational tests most
commonly cited as evidence for expansion: Type~Ia supernova
light-curve time dilation and the Tolman surface brightness test.
Both follow from the Etherington relation $D_L = (1+z)\,D_A$, which
holds in the metric~\eqref{eq:metric} exactly
[Eqs.~\eqref{eq:etherington}--\eqref{eq:tolman}].  These tests
distinguish UDT from models with no redshift or no time dilation ---
they do not distinguish between a static metric with a spatial
$\phi$-gradient and an expanding metric with a temporal scale factor.

Whether the universe is expanding or static is an observational
question, and we note that the genuinely decisive test is cosmic drift
spectroscopy: the Sandage--Loeb
test~\cite{Sandage1962,Loeb1998} --- a model-independent measurement
of whether cosmological redshifts change with time --- has not yet
been performed at the required sensitivity.  UDT predicts zero drift;
the expanding universe predicts nonzero drift detectable by ELT on a
10--20~year baseline.

\subsection{The role of $\phi$}

The scalar field $\phi(r)$ plays a dual role:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Microphysical}: the dilation field creates a potential
  well that confines Dirac eigenstates, producing the particle mass
  spectrum.
\item \textbf{Cosmological}: the same field, integrated over
  cosmological distances, produces the redshift-distance relation,
  CMB peak positions, and BAO structure.
\end{enumerate}
The two regimes are cleanly separated: the microphysical cavity has
$\rstar \approx 7$ in natural units ($\sim 7$~fm), while the
cosmological boundary has $\rstar \approx 10$~Gpc.  The same equation
[Eq.~\eqref{eq:kg}] governs both, with different parameters
($\mu$ vs.\ $\mug$) and different sources.

\subsection{Gravitational waves}

The UDT action is $S_\mathrm{EH} + S_\phi$ (Einstein--Hilbert plus
minimally coupled massive scalar).  $S_\mathrm{EH}$ is not imported:
the verified field equations ($G^\theta_{\ \theta} = -\Box_g\phi$ to
$10^{-14}$, Bianchi identity to $10^{-9}$) \emph{are} the Einstein
equations, whose action is $S_\mathrm{EH}$.

Perturbing $g_{\mu\nu} \to \bar{g}_{\mu\nu} + h_{\mu\nu}$ and
$\phi \to \phi_0 + \delta\phi$, the second variation $\delta^2 S$
decomposes into orthogonal sectors.  The cross terms vanish because
$\partial^\mu\phi_0$ is purely radial on the spherically symmetric
background, and the TT projection of any tensor contracted with a
radial vector is identically zero.  The result:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Scalar sector} (from $S_\phi$):
  $\Box\,\delta\phi = \mu^2\,\delta\phi$ --- massive, with threshold
  frequency $f_\mathrm{thresh} = 3.5 \times 10^{22}$~Hz
  ($10^{19}\times$ LIGO band).  Kinematically forbidden from
  radiating at astrophysical frequencies.
\item \textbf{Tensor sector} (from $S_\mathrm{EH}$):
  $\Box\,h^{TT}_{\mu\nu} = -16\pi G\,T^{TT}_{\mu\nu}$ --- massless,
  standard GR.  The Peters quadrupole formula follows:
  $P = P_\mathrm{GR} \times [1 + O(\phi^2)]$.
\end{itemize}
The scalar channel is suppressed; the tensor channel reduces to GR.  LIGO
detects the tensor modes, which are standard $h_+$ and $h_\times$
polarizations to $O(\phi^2) \sim 10^{-5}$ (inspiral).  At merger
($\phi \sim 0.1$), the $O(\phi^2) \sim 1\%$ correction may be
accessible to Cosmic Explorer and Einstein Telescope --- a falsifiable
prediction.

The screening mass $\mu^2 = \pi/3$ is the \emph{same} mass that
determines particle eigenvalues and nuclear confinement.  There is one
field equation at all scales; the cosmological $\mug = \pi\mu/13$ is a
scale in the polynomial solution, not a mass in the equation.

\subsection{The Hubble tension}

In this framework, the Hubble constant $H_0$ is not a fundamental
parameter.  The distance-redshift relation is determined by the
$\phi$ profile, not by an expansion rate.  The ``Hubble tension'' ---
the disagreement~\cite{Riess2022,Verde2019} between early-universe and late-universe
measurements of $H_0$ --- dissolves: there is no single $H_0$ to
measure, and the apparent tension arises from fitting a
redshift-dependent $\phi$ profile with a constant expansion rate.

\subsection{The shortest paper in physics}

In 1951, Friedrich Lenz published~\cite{Lenz1951} what is widely regarded as the
shortest paper in the history of \textit{Physical Review}
(Fig.~\ref{fig:lenz}).  Its entire content: ``The most exact value at
present for the ratio of proton to electron mass is
$1836.12 \pm 0.05$.  It may be of interest to note that this number
coincides with $6\pi^5 = 1836.12$.''

For seventy-five years, this coincidence had no explanation.  The
Standard Model treats $m_p/m_e$ as an input --- the proton mass
emerges from QCD confinement dynamics and bears no known algebraic
relationship to $\pi$.  Lenz's observation was filed alongside other
``numerological curiosities'' and largely forgotten.

The framework presented here provides the derivation.  The
proton mass formula
$m_p = (2j+1)(2\ell+1)\,\pi^{2|\kappa_\mathrm{max}|-1}\,m_e
= 6\pi^5\,m_e$ follows from the angular coupling of spinor harmonics
on $S^2$, where the coefficient $6 = (2j+1)(2\ell+1)$ is the
spin-orbital multiplicity and the power $5 = 2|\kappa_\mathrm{max}|-1$
is the angular content of the closure orbit.  The coincidence that
Lenz noted is exact: $6\pi^5 = 1836.118$, compared to the current
PDG value $m_p/m_e = 1836.153$ ($-0.002\%$).  The shortest paper in
physics history now has an answer.

A natural objection is that integer-$\pi$ combinations can be found to
approximate almost any dimensionless ratio, making such agreements
numerologically suspect.  The framework responds on three grounds.
First, the integers $(2,3,5,7)$ are not chosen for their primality ---
they are forced as the unique solution to the self-consistency
condition of spin-$1/2$ fermions on~$S^2$
(Sec.~\ref{sec:angular}, Table~\ref{tab:diophantine_scan}).  Second,
the derivation chain is top-down: the Diophantine triple fixes the
integers, and the mass formulas follow; no mass ratio is fitted and
then reverse-engineered to an integer combination.  Third, the
framework has genuine predictive power over previously unknown or
unexplained quantities: $\delta_{\rm CP}^{\rm PMNS} = \pi +
\arcsin(4/13) = 197.9°$ (within $0.04\sigma$ of measurement),
$\Delta m^2_{21} = 6.98 \times 10^{-5}$~eV$^2$ (between the
solar-only and reactor measurements, testable by JUNO), and the CMB
even/odd asymmetry coefficient $6/13 = 2\sin^2\!\theta_W$ (connecting
particle physics to cosmology with no free parameter).  A
numerological framework has no predictive power --- it explains only
what it is fitted to.  This framework derives both known and unknown
quantities from the same three quantum numbers.

\section{The Vacuum Classification Theorem}\label{app:vacuum}
%===================================================================

Define $y(r) := e^{-2\phi(r)} > 0$.  The vacuum flux system is
equivalent to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:yode}
  y'' + \frac{2}{r}\,y' = \mu^2\ln y\,,\quad
  y(0) = e^{-2\phiz},\quad y'(0) = 0\,.
\end{equation}

\textbf{Theorem.} For $\mu > 0$ and regular origin data:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] $\phiz > 0$: $y$ reaches zero at finite $r_c$
  ($\phi \to +\infty$, black hole horizon).
\item[(ii)] $\phiz = 0$: $y \equiv 1$, $\phi \equiv 0$ (trivial).
\item[(iii)] $\phiz < 0$: $y$ is globally defined, strictly
  increasing, $y \to \infty$ ($\phi \to -\infty$).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Proof.}  Case~(i): $y_0 < 1$, so $\ln y < 0$, driving $y$
downward.  The quadratic bound
$y(r) \leq y_0 - (\mu^2 a/6)\,r^2$ with $a = -\ln y_0 > 0$
guarantees $y = 0$ at finite $r_c$.  Case~(iii): $y_0 > 1$, so
$\ln y > 0$, driving $y$ upward.  Global existence follows from
$y(r) \leq y_0\exp(\mu^2 r^2/6)$.  A finite limit $L > 1$ leads to
$y' \sim cr$ for large $r$, contradicting convergence.  $\square$

\section{The Separatrix Theorem}\label{app:separatrix}
%===================================================================

For the sourced scalar equation with $\varepsilon$-decomposition
$S(r) = \mu^2\phi(1 - \varepsilon)$, the \emph{null-source curve}
satisfies $J' = 0$ everywhere.  Setting $u = \phi'(r)$ gives a
Bernoulli ODE with solution:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:separatrix}
  \phi'_{\mathrm{sep}}(r) = \frac{1}{r(2 + Cr)}\,,
\end{equation}
where $C$ is an integration constant (distinct from the mass
calibration).  Any profile with $\phi'$ above this curve is in the
drive regime; below is in the brake regime.  The sign-change radius
$r_{sc}$ (maximum of the scalar flux) satisfies
$J'(r_{sc}) = 0$ and is determined entirely by the profile
coefficients, with no external input.

\section{Flux Formulation and Vacuum Classification}\label{app:flux}
%===================================================================

We define the radial flux $J(r) \equiv r^2 e^{-2\phi}\,\phi'$.  The
scalar equation~\eqref{eq:kg} then takes the canonical first-order form:
% VERIFIED: CG §8.1.1
\begin{align}\label{eq:flux}
  J'(r) &= r^2\bigl(\mu^2\,\phi - S(r)\bigr)\,, \nonumber\\
  \phi'(r) &= \frac{J(r)\,e^{2\phi(r)}}{r^2}\,.
\end{align}
Regularity at the origin requires $J(0) = 0$ (equivalently
$\phi'(0) = 0$), so the local initial-value problem is specified by
$\phi(0) = \phiz$ and the source $S(r)$.

For vacuum ($S = 0$) with $\mu > 0$, the solution space is completely
classified by $\mathrm{sign}(\phiz)$~\cite{zenodo2025}:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\phiz > 0$: $\phi(r) \to +\infty$ at finite $r_c$
  (black hole branch, Sec.~\ref{sec:blackholes}).
\item $\phiz = 0$: trivial solution $\phi \equiv 0$.
\item $\phiz < 0$: global monotone decrease, $\phi(r) \to -\infty$
  as $r \to \infty$ (particle branch).
\end{itemize}
